## What Driven by STEM Is

Driven by STEM is a MakeCode Arcade learning experience that uses an F1-inspired racing challenge to make engineering decisions visible through action. Students are not only driving a car. They are making choices, testing them, reading feedback, and improving based on what the system shows them.

The project is built around a simple but important idea: good engineering is not about making one number as large as possible. It is about balancing performance, efficiency, and strategy under constraints.

## What Learners Actually Experience

Learners move through a guided skillmap rather than opening a blank coding workspace.

- In **The Garage**, they enter the mission, make setup choices, and run a first shakedown.
- In **On the Road**, they feel those choices in motion, encounter pit stop briefings, and adapt to changing conditions.
- In **The Finish Line**, they apply what they have learned, review the results, and connect the experience back to careers and next steps.

This structure matters because it keeps the session coherent. Students are not collecting disconnected activities. They are moving through one arc that starts with curiosity, becomes active decision-making, and ends with reflection.

## Why It Works in a Classroom or Live Event

- feedback is immediate enough that students can see what a change did without waiting for a long explanation
- the experience rewards comparison and discussion, not just winning
- the MakeCode Arcade format lowers setup friction and keeps the project remixable
- the same path can support short demos, full lessons, or longer facilitated activations
- the game gives educators natural pause points for asking what changed, why it changed, and which role would care

## What Educators Should Expect

Students will spend most of their time in a build-test-improve loop. They will make a decision, run the game, notice what changed, and explain what they think caused it. The educator's job is to guide attention toward evidence, not to narrate every mechanic.

That means the most useful facilitation moves are usually short:

- point to one visible change
- ask for one prediction before a test
- ask students what became easier, harder, faster, riskier, or more efficient
- connect that observation to a role, system, or design choice

## What Success Looks Like

You are on the right track if learners can do most of the following in simple language:

- name one change they made or noticed
- describe what happened next in the game
- explain one tradeoff instead of focusing only on speed
- connect a gameplay moment to a real team role such as software engineer, strategist, telemetry analyst, designer, or sustainability lead

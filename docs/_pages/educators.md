This guide is for educators, facilitators, and event leads who want more than a launch link. It is here to help you understand what learners will actually experience, where the most important teaching moments happen, and how to keep the session feeling active, clear, and worth discussing.

If you are opening the project for the first time, do not try to memorize everything. Start with the quick-start sequence below, then use the rest of the guide as needed.

## Quick Start

1. Read the [Summary]({{ site.baseurl }}/educators/summary/) page for the short version of what the project is and why it works.
2. Review [Requirements]({{ site.baseurl }}/educators/requirements/) so the room, devices, and links are ready before learners arrive.
3. Open [Goals]({{ site.baseurl }}/educators/goals/) and [Foundations]({{ site.baseurl }}/educators/foundations/) to frame the learning and the tradeoff model.
4. Use [Agenda]({{ site.baseurl }}/educators/agenda/) and [Run of Show]({{ site.baseurl }}/educators/run-of-show/) when you are preparing to deliver the session live.
5. Keep [Definitions]({{ site.baseurl }}/educators/definitions/), [Accessibility]({{ site.baseurl }}/educators/accessibility/), and [Feedback]({{ site.baseurl }}/educators/feedback/) nearby during facilitation.

## What Learners Do

Driven by STEM uses a MakeCode Arcade skillmap with three stages.

### Stage 1: The Garage

Learners enter as a junior innovation team, understand the mission, make early setup choices, and run a first shakedown test. This is where they first experience the idea that there is no single perfect choice.

### Stage 2: On the Road

Learners take those choices into active gameplay. Obstacles, pit stop briefings, and changing conditions help them connect what they are seeing on screen to real roles in software, strategy, data, design, and operations.

### Stage 3: The Finish Line

Learners apply everything in a final challenge, then review what happened and close on a note that values thinking, revision, and growth rather than a simple win-or-lose result.

## What This Guide Helps You Do

- launch the experience without a long setup explanation
- point students toward visible cause-and-effect moments
- keep tradeoffs between performance, efficiency, and strategy at the center
- connect the game to real STEM and career pathways without making the session feel like a lecture
- choose good pause points for compare-and-explain discussion
- support students who are new to coding, shared-device play, or technical vocabulary

## Best-Fit Session Shapes

### 20-30 Minute Demo or Activation Pass

Use a shorter introduction, one visible build or test moment, one compare-and-explain pause, and a spoken reflection at the end.

### 45-60 Minute Classroom Session

This is the best fit for most educators. Students have time to move through the core skillmap path, notice outcomes, and reflect on one meaningful tradeoff.

### 90-Minute Facilitated Activation

This version leaves room for more guided discussion, stronger career connections, and one or two remix moments after the core path is complete.

## Core Principles for Educators

- keep the opening short so students get to action quickly
- ask learners to predict before they test whenever possible
- treat unexpected results as useful data
- point to one variable, block, or score lens at a time
- keep the conversation grounded in what the game actually showed
- remind students that thoughtful, observant play is just as valuable as fast play

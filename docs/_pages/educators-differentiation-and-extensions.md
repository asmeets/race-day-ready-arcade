Use this section to keep the experience readable, inclusive, and reliable in classrooms and live events.

## Readability and Clarity

- keep instructions short enough to read on a projector without strain
- introduce one mechanic at a time before layering in discussion about strategy or careers
- use device-neutral action words such as select, choose, press, and test
- make the next useful action obvious after a mistake, reset, or weak result
- avoid long paragraphs on screen when a short label or one spoken sentence will do

## Multiple Cues

Important events should be readable in more than one way.

- use motion, score, text, and sound together when possible
- avoid relying on color alone to show success, risk, or urgency
- make state changes obvious enough that a student can describe what happened without needing perfect hearing or a front-row seat
- keep celebration and warning feedback distinct so students do not have to guess what a signal means

## Participation Supports

The project works well with shared-device play when the roles are explicit.

- use a **Driver** role for controls and a **Navigator** role for observation, reading, and explanation
- rotate roles at a natural checkpoint such as the end of a shakedown, pit stop, or replay
- provide sentence starters when students need support with reflection, such as "We changed...", "We noticed...", or "Next time we would..."
- pause for a whole-group compare-and-explain moment before independent remixing if the room needs a reset

## Belonging and Voice

Accessibility is not only about technical access. It is also about making the room feel like a place where different strengths matter.

- affirm observation, patience, communication, and revision as real STEM strengths
- make it clear that students do not need prior coding experience to contribute meaningfully
- avoid framing strong performance as something only the fastest or most confident learners can achieve
- connect roles to different kinds of thinking so more students can see themselves in the work

## Reliability Notes

- keep a fallback demo ready in case a learner device stalls or loses connection
- choose shared-device roles that are easy to explain and rotate
- prefer short retry loops so mistakes feel recoverable instead of punishing
- use the skillmap as the primary path because it gives a clearer on-ramp than opening the full project first

## Quick Accessibility Check

Before the session begins, ask yourself:

- Can the room read the main text and score areas?
- Can a student understand the next step without a long explanation?
- Are key events visible without relying on one single cue?
- Does the session still work if devices need to be shared?

This section outlines the detailed facilitation moves for a classroom lesson, workshop, or live activation.

## Before Learners Begin

Set the tone early. The opening should communicate that this is a problem-solving session, not a quiz and not a lecture.

- launch the skillmap before students settle in
- explain the challenge in under a minute
- define tradeoff in plain language, such as "improving one thing can make another thing harder"
- establish device roles if students are sharing, such as Driver and Navigator

## Recommended 45-60 Minute Facilitation Flow

### 1. Launch the Challenge

- introduce the idea that racing depends on tradeoffs, not just speed
- show the skillmap and tell learners they will build, test, compare, and improve
- preview one or two roles they will hear about later, such as strategist, software engineer, or telemetry analyst

Suggested educator language:

"Today you are not trying to find one perfect answer. You are going to make choices, test them, and decide what those choices made better or harder."

### 2. Guide Stage 1: The Garage

- keep the opening steps moving so students reach a first visible success quickly
- point out the three score lenses when they appear, but do not over-explain them
- ask for a prediction before the first shakedown run
- use the Garage test as the first compare-and-explain moment

Useful questions:

- What do you think this setup will help with?
- What might it cost you later?
- What should we watch for on the test run?

### 3. Guide Stage 2: On the Road

- let the motion and changing conditions do some of the teaching for you
- pause at one visible moment where the earlier setup clearly helped or created a problem
- use pit stop briefings to connect gameplay to roles without turning the session into a lecture
- ask students what changed once the car left the Garage

Useful questions:

- Which earlier choice is helping you now?
- Which one is creating a new problem?
- When is the fastest choice not the smartest choice?
- Which team role would care most about this moment?

### 4. Guide Stage 3: The Finish Line

- frame the final challenge as a chance to apply learning, not prove worth
- remind students to watch performance, efficiency, and strategy together
- keep the reflection short and concrete after the final run
- leave time for one next-step statement instead of a long written explanation

Useful questions:

- What choice had the biggest effect on your result?
- What would you change if you ran it again?
- Which tradeoff mattered most by the end?

## Educator Moves That Help

- point to one variable, block, or score lens at a time
- ask learners to predict before they test
- treat unexpected results as useful data
- keep discussions grounded in what the game actually showed
- keep prompts short enough that students can return quickly to action
- invite more than one right answer when students are comparing strategies

## Common Places to Pause

- after the first setup choice in The Garage
- after the Garage shakedown result screen
- at the first pit stop or career briefing moment
- when changing conditions force learners to adapt
- right after the final challenge before students move into closure

## Tips for Live Events

- use shorter rounds with faster resets
- prioritize highly visible changes over long explanations
- keep a fallback demo ready in case connectivity is uneven
- if time collapses, keep one compare-and-explain pause and one closing reflection rather than trying to rush every node
